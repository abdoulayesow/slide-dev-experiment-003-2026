## CHAPTER 10: SCRUM MASTER AS COACH (Slides 6-18)

**Slide 6: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "10"
- Title: "Scrum Master as Coach"
- **Visual:** Full-screen gradient background

**Slide 7: What is Servant Leadership?**
- Title: "Servant Leadership - The Scrum Master's Foundation"
- Content:
  - **Traditional Leadership:** Authority, command, control
  - **Servant Leadership:** Service, empowerment, facilitation
  - **Key Shift:** "How can I help you succeed?" vs "Do what I say"
  - **Scrum Master Focus:**
    - Removing impediments
    - Creating environment for success
    - Coaching and developing others
    - Leading by example
- **Visual:** Comparison diagram, servant leader vs traditional leader

**Slide 8: Servant Leadership in Action**
- Title: "What Servant Leadership Looks Like"
- Content:
  - **Instead of:** Telling team what to do
  - **You do:** Ask questions that help them discover solutions
  - **Instead of:** Solving all problems yourself
  - **You do:** Teach team to solve their own problems
  - **Instead of:** Taking credit for successes
  - **You do:** Celebrate team achievements
  - **Instead of:** Blaming for failures
  - **You do:** Help team learn from mistakes
- **Visual:** Side-by-side comparison cards

**Slide 9: Coaching vs Mentoring vs Managing**
- Title: "Understanding Different Approaches"
- Content:
  - **Coaching:**
    - Asking powerful questions
    - Helping people find their own answers
    - Focus on self-discovery
    - Example: "What options do you see?"
  - **Mentoring:**
    - Sharing your experience
    - Giving advice from your journey
    - Focus on guidance
    - Example: "When I faced this, I tried..."
  - **Managing:**
    - Directing work
    - Making decisions
    - Focus on outcomes
    - Example: "You need to do X by Friday"
  - **Scrum Master:** Primarily coaches, sometimes mentors, rarely manages
- **Visual:** Three columns with distinct icons

**Slide 10: Powerful Questions**
- Title: "The Art of Powerful Questions"
- Subtitle: "Questions that unlock thinking"
- Content:
  - **What Makes a Question Powerful?**
    - Open-ended (not yes/no)
    - Thought-provoking
    - Focused on learning/growth
    - Non-judgmental
  - **Examples:**
    - "What would success look like?"
    - "What's blocking progress?"
    - "What have you tried so far?"
    - "What would need to be true for this to work?"
    - "How will you know you've succeeded?"
    - "What will you do differently next time?"
- **Visual:** Question mark icon, example questions in cards

**Slide 11: Coaching Question Frameworks**
- Title: "Question Frameworks for Different Situations"
- Content:
  - **When Team is Stuck:**
    - "What have we tried?"
    - "What else could we try?"
    - "Who could help us?"
  - **When There's Conflict:**
    - "What's important to each person?"
    - "What would a good outcome look like?"
    - "How can we test different approaches?"
  - **When Goals Unclear:**
    - "What are we trying to achieve?"
    - "How will we know we're successful?"
    - "What's the most important thing?"
  - **When Progress Slow:**
    - "What's slowing us down?"
    - "What could we stop doing?"
    - "What would help us move faster?"
- **Visual:** Four scenario cards with sample questions

**Slide 12: Active Listening**
- Title: "Active Listening - The Foundation of Coaching"
- Content:
  - **What is Active Listening?**
    - Fully concentrating on speaker
    - Understanding their message
    - Responding thoughtfully
    - Remembering what was said
  - **Techniques:**
    - Maintain eye contact
    - Don't interrupt
    - Paraphrase to confirm understanding ("So what I'm hearing is...")
    - Ask clarifying questions
    - Notice body language and tone
    - Put away distractions
  - **What NOT to Do:**
    - ‚ùå Thinking about your response while they talk
    - ‚ùå Jumping to solutions immediately
    - ‚ùå Checking your phone
    - ‚ùå Finishing their sentences
- **Visual:** Do's and Don'ts comparison

**Slide 13: The Coaching Conversation Flow**
- Title: "Structure of a Coaching Conversation"
- Content:
  - **1. Set Context (2 min)**
    - What do we need to discuss?
    - What's the goal of this conversation?
  - **2. Explore Situation (5-10 min)**
    - What's happening?
    - How is it affecting you/the team?
    - What have you tried?
  - **3. Generate Options (5-10 min)**
    - What could you do?
    - What else?
    - What if...?
  - **4. Commit to Action (3-5 min)**
    - What will you do?
    - By when?
    - How will you know it worked?
  - **5. Follow Up**
    - Check in later
    - Support as needed
- **Visual:** Flow diagram with timeline

**Slide 14: Feedback - The Coaching Tool**
- Title: "Giving Effective Feedback"
- Content:
  - **SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact):**
    - **Situation:** When/where it happened
    - **Behavior:** What you observed (specific, not judgment)
    - **Impact:** Effect on you/team/project
  - **Example - Positive:**
    - Situation: "In yesterday's Daily Scrum..."
    - Behavior: "...you suggested we pair on the API issue..."
    - Impact: "...which helped us unblock the work and James learned a new skill"
  - **Example - Constructive:**
    - Situation: "In Sprint Planning..."
    - Behavior: "...the discussion about story sizing went 30 minutes over timebox..."
    - Impact: "...which left no time to discuss dependencies, and we're hitting blockers now"
  - **Key:** Be specific, timely, and focus on behavior (not personality)
- **Visual:** SBI model diagram with examples

**Slide 15: Conflict Resolution**
- Title: "Navigating Conflicts as Scrum Master"
- Content:
  - **Types of Healthy Conflict:**
    - ‚úÖ Disagreement about approach
    - ‚úÖ Debate about priorities
    - ‚úÖ Discussion about quality standards
  - **Types of Unhealthy Conflict:**
    - ‚ùå Personal attacks
    - ‚ùå Blaming
    - ‚ùå Power struggles
  - **Your Role as Scrum Master:**
    - Create safe space for disagreement
    - Focus on issues, not personalities
    - Help find common ground
    - Facilitate conversation (don't solve it for them)
  - **When to Intervene:**
    - Conflict becomes personal
    - Team can't resolve on their own
    - Work is being blocked
- **Visual:** Healthy vs unhealthy conflict comparison

**Slide 16: Conflict Resolution Techniques**
- Title: "Techniques for Resolving Conflicts"
- Content:
  - **1. Private Conversations First**
    - Talk to each person individually
    - Understand their perspective
    - Don't take sides
  - **2. Focus on Interests, Not Positions**
    - Position: "I want to use React"
    - Interest: "I want maintainable code"
    - Find solutions that meet interests
  - **3. Explore Options Together**
    - "What if we tried...?"
    - "Could we experiment with...?"
    - Test and inspect
  - **4. Agree on Experiment**
    - Try approach for one Sprint
    - Review in Retrospective
    - Adapt based on learning
- **Visual:** Step-by-step process

**Slide 17: Difficult Conversations**
- Title: "Having Difficult Conversations"
- Content:
  - **When You Need to Address:**
    - Performance issues
    - Disruptive behavior
    - Lack of engagement
    - Violating Scrum values
  - **Preparation:**
    - Be clear on the issue
    - Have specific examples
    - Choose private setting
    - Time it appropriately
  - **During Conversation:**
    - Start with positive intent
    - State observations (not judgments)
    - Listen to their perspective
    - Collaborate on solutions
    - Follow up
  - **Example Opening:**
    - "I've noticed that in the last three Daily Scrums, you've been on your phone. This is important because it signals to others that the meeting doesn't matter. Can you help me understand what's going on?"
- **Visual:** Conversation framework

**Slide 18: Coaching the Coach**
- Title: "Continuous Learning as a Coach"
- Content:
  - **Self-Reflection:**
    - What went well in my coaching today?
    - What could I improve?
    - Did I ask or tell?
    - Did I listen fully?
  - **Seek Feedback:**
    - Ask team for feedback on your facilitation
    - Find a mentor or coach
    - Join Scrum Master communities
  - **Practice:**
    - Role-play difficult scenarios
    - Record yourself facilitating
    - Try new techniques
  - **Remember:** You're on a learning journey too
- **Visual:** Continuous improvement cycle

## CHAPTER 11: SUPPORTING THE PRODUCT OWNER (Slides 19-27)

**Slide 19: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "11"
- Title: "Supporting the Product Owner"
- **Visual:** Full-screen gradient background

**Slide 20: The Product Owner's Challenge**
- Title: "Understanding PO Pressures"
- Content:
  - **PO is Pulled in Many Directions:**
    - Stakeholders want everything NOW
    - Developers need clear priorities
    - CEO wants revenue
    - Users want features
    - Market is changing
  - **Common PO Struggles:**
    - Saying "no" to good ideas
    - Managing stakeholder expectations
    - Keeping backlog refined
    - Balancing short-term vs long-term
    - Making tough prioritization calls
  - **Your Role:** Coach and support, not do their job for them
- **Visual:** PO in center with arrows from different stakeholders

**Slide 21: Coaching PO on Product Goal**
- Title: "Helping Define a Clear Product Goal"
- Content:
  - **What is a Product Goal?**
    - Long-term objective for the product
    - One at a time
    - Provides focus for all Sprints
  - **Powerful Questions to Ask:**
    - "What does success look like in 6 months?"
    - "What outcome are we trying to achieve?"
    - "How will we know we've succeeded?"
    - "What's the most important thing users need?"
  - **Red Flags:**
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Product Goal is too vague ("Build a great app")
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è No Product Goal at all
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Product Goal changes every Sprint
  - **Help PO:** Make it specific, measurable, inspiring
- **Visual:** Product Goal framework

**Slide 22: Product Backlog Management**
- Title: "Supporting Effective Backlog Management"
- Content:
  - **Help PO With:**
    - Refinement techniques
    - Ordering by value
    - Writing clear User Stories
    - Estimation facilitation
    - Transparency to stakeholders
  - **Backlog Refinement:**
    - Ongoing activity (not an event)
    - ~10% of team capacity
    - PO leads, team participates
    - Focus on upcoming Sprints
  - **Signs of Healthy Backlog:**
    - ‚úÖ Top items are refined and ready
    - ‚úÖ Order reflects value/priority
    - ‚úÖ Items have clear acceptance criteria
    - ‚úÖ Team understands upcoming work
- **Visual:** Healthy vs unhealthy backlog comparison

**Slide 23: Stakeholder Management**
- Title: "Coaching PO on Stakeholder Engagement"
- Content:
  - **Common PO Challenge:** Stakeholders bypass PO and go directly to Developers
  - **Your Coaching:**
    - "How can you make stakeholders feel heard?"
    - "What channels exist for feedback?"
    - "How often do you engage stakeholders?"
  - **Strategies to Suggest:**
    - Regular stakeholder updates
    - Involve key stakeholders in Sprint Review
    - Share Product Goal and roadmap
    - Be transparent about trade-offs
  - **When Escalation Needed:**
    - Stakeholder consistently undermines PO
    - Developers are being pulled in different directions
    - Organization doesn't respect PO role
- **Visual:** Stakeholder engagement strategies

**Slide 24: Value Maximization**
- Title: "Helping PO Maximize Value"
- Content:
  - **Value-Based Ordering:**
    - Not all features are equal
    - Consider: user value, business value, risk reduction, learning
    - Use value vs effort matrix
  - **Questions to Ask:**
    - "What delivers the most value to users?"
    - "What reduces the most risk?"
    - "What helps us learn the most?"
    - "What are we optimizing for?"
  - **Help PO Avoid:**
    - Feature factory (building everything requested)
    - HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion)
    - Analysis paralysis
  - **Encourage:** Data-driven decisions, user feedback, experimentation
- **Visual:** Value/Effort matrix

**Slide 25: When PO Struggles**
- Title: "Common PO Anti-Patterns & Your Response"
- Content:
  - **Anti-Pattern: PO is unavailable**
    - Impact: Team blocked, delays
    - Your coaching: "How can we ensure you're available when team needs you?"
  - **Anti-Pattern: PO can't say no**
    - Impact: Overcommitted, unfocused
    - Your coaching: "What happens when we say yes to everything?"
  - **Anti-Pattern: PO micromanages Developers**
    - Impact: Team isn't self-managing
    - Your coaching: "What outcome do you want? Can team decide how?"
  - **Anti-Pattern: PO doesn't attend events**
    - Impact: Missing input, unclear priorities
    - Your coaching: "What would help you prioritize these events?"
- **Visual:** Four anti-pattern cards

**Slide 26: Product Owner by Committee**
- Title: "When There Are Multiple 'Product Owners'"
- Content:
  - **The Problem:**
    - Organization has committee make product decisions
    - Multiple people giving Developers direction
    - No single accountable person
  - **Why This Fails:**
    - Slow decision-making
    - Conflicting priorities
    - Developers don't know who to listen to
    - No clear ownership
  - **Your Role:**
    - Educate organization on PO accountability
    - Help identify one person as THE Product Owner
    - Coach that person to gather input but make decisions
  - **Remember:** PO can delegate work, but stays accountable
- **Visual:** Committee chaos vs single PO clarity

**Slide 27: Supporting PO Success**
- Title: "Creating Conditions for PO Success"
- Content:
  - **As Scrum Master, You Can:**
    - Facilitate backlog refinement
    - Coach on Scrum framework
    - Help with stakeholder engagement
    - Protect from interruptions during Sprint
    - Provide feedback on effectiveness
  - **You Cannot:**
    - ‚ùå Make product decisions
    - ‚ùå Order the Product Backlog
    - ‚ùå Define Product Goal
    - ‚ùå Represent users
  - **Key:** Enable PO to succeed in their role
- **Visual:** Support framework diagram

    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Scrum Master assigns tasks

    - Unclear requirements
    - Tool/environment issues

    2. Determine if team can self-resolve
    3. If not, take ownership of removal
    4. Keep team updated on progress
    5. Escalate if needed

## CHAPTER 13: METRICS & MEASUREMENT (Slides 37-47)

**Slide 37: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "13"
- Title: "Metrics That Matter"
- **Visual:** Full-screen gradient background

**Slide 38: Why Metrics?**
- Title: "The Purpose of Metrics in Scrum"
- Content:
  - **Good Reasons for Metrics:**
    - ‚úÖ Transparency (make work visible)
    - ‚úÖ Inspection (understand current state)
    - ‚úÖ Adaptation (identify improvements)
    - ‚úÖ Forecasting (predict completion)
  - **Bad Reasons for Metrics:**
    - ‚ùå Comparing teams
    - ‚ùå Judging individuals
    - ‚ùå Punishment
    - ‚ùå Micromanagement
  - **Remember:** Metrics are for the team to improve, not for management to control
- **Visual:** Good vs bad uses of metrics

**Slide 39: Velocity**
- Title: "Understanding Velocity"
- Content:
  - **What is Velocity?**
    - Amount of work team completes per Sprint
    - Measured in story points
    - Team-specific (can't compare across teams)
  - **How to Use It:**
    - ‚úÖ Forecast future work
    - ‚úÖ Understand team capacity
    - ‚úÖ Identify unusual variations
    - ‚úÖ Plan Sprints realistically
  - **How NOT to Use It:**
    - ‚ùå Performance metric ("Team A has higher velocity!")
    - ‚ùå Pressure to increase ("Why did velocity drop?!")
    - ‚ùå Commitment tool ("You must hit 30 points!")
  - **Important:** Velocity typically stabilizes after 3-5 Sprints
- **Visual:** Velocity chart over time

**Slide 40: Velocity Variations**
- Title: "What Affects Velocity?"
- Content:
  - **Expected Variations:**
    - New team (velocity stabilizing)
    - Holidays/vacation
    - Learning new technology
    - Adding/removing team members
  - **Concerning Variations:**
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Steady decline (technical debt? morale?)
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Wild swings (unclear estimates? scope issues?)
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Artificially inflated (gaming the system?)
  - **What to Do:**
    - Discuss in Retrospective
    - Look for patterns
    - Focus on improvement, not blame
- **Visual:** Velocity trends with annotations

**Slide 41: Burndown Chart**
- Title: "Sprint Burndown Chart"
- Content:
  - **What It Shows:**
    - Work remaining vs time
    - Are we on track to complete Sprint?
  - **How to Read It:**
    - Y-axis: Story points or tasks remaining
    - X-axis: Days in Sprint
    - Ideal line: Straight diagonal
    - Actual line: Team's real progress
  - **Patterns to Notice:**
    - Flat line early = slow start
    - Staying above ideal = at risk
    - Sudden drop = work completed in batch
  - **Use For:** Daily inspection, not judgment
- **Visual:** Sample burndown chart with patterns

**Slide 42: Burnup Chart**
- Title: "Burnup Chart - Alternative View"
- Content:
  - **What It Shows:**
    - Work completed vs total scope
    - How scope changes over time
  - **Advantage Over Burndown:**
    - Shows scope changes clearly
    - Progress always goes up (psychologically better)
    - Better for release planning
  - **How to Read:**
    - Bottom line: Completed work (always increasing)
    - Top line: Total scope (can change)
    - Gap: Work remaining
  - **When to Use:** Multi-Sprint or release planning
- **Visual:** Burnup chart example

**Slide 43: Definition of Done**
- Title: "Definition of Done as Quality Metric"
- Content:
  - **What is Definition of Done (DoD)?**
    - Shared understanding of "complete"
    - Checklist for every Increment
    - Non-negotiable quality standard
  - **Example DoD:**
    - ‚úì Code written and reviewed
    - ‚úì Unit tests passing
    - ‚úì Integration tests passing
    - ‚úì Documentation updated
    - ‚úì Acceptance criteria met
    - ‚úì Deployed to staging
  - **Why It Matters:**
    - Ensures consistent quality
    - Prevents technical debt
    - Enables sustainable pace
  - **Your Role:** Help team create and maintain strong DoD
- **Visual:** DoD checklist example

**Slide 44: Cumulative Flow Diagram**
- Title: "Cumulative Flow Diagram (Advanced)"
- Content:
  - **What It Shows:**
    - Work in different states over time
    - Workflow bottlenecks
  - **How to Read:**
    - Each colored band = stage (To Do, In Progress, Done)
    - Width of band = amount of work in that state
    - Widening band = bottleneck
  - **Use For:**
    - Identifying process problems
    - Understanding flow efficiency
  - **When to Use:** When team is mature and wants deeper insights
- **Visual:** CFD diagram with annotations

**Slide 45: Metrics Misuse Warning**
- Title: "‚ö†Ô∏è When Metrics Become Harmful"
- Content:
  - **Goodhart's Law:**
    - "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"
  - **Examples of Gaming:**
    - Inflating story points to show higher velocity
    - Declaring work "done" when it's not
    - Cherry-picking easy work to boost metrics
  - **Signs of Metric Abuse:**
    - Team focuses on metric instead of value
    - Fear around reporting numbers
    - Metrics used for punishment
    - Metrics don't drive improvement
  - **Solution:** Use metrics for learning, not judging
- **Visual:** Warning signs

**Slide 46: The Metrics Dashboard**
- Title: "What to Track and Share"
- Content:
  - **Recommended for Most Teams:**
    - Sprint Goal (qualitative)
    - Velocity (trend over time)
    - Sprint Burndown (daily tracking)
    - Definition of Done compliance
    - Impediments (count and age)
  - **Optional/Advanced:**
    - Cumulative Flow
    - Cycle time
    - Defect rate
    - Team happiness
