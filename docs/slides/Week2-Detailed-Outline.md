## CHAPTER 6: SPRINT PLANNING DEEP DIVE (Slides 6-18)

**Slide 6: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "6"
- Title: "Sprint Planning Mastery"

**Slide 7: Sprint Planning Structure**
- Title: "The Two Parts of Sprint Planning"
- Content:
  - **Part 1: Why & What (First Half)**
    - Product Owner presents Product Goal
    - Team discusses highest-value items
    - Select items for Sprint
    - Craft Sprint Goal together
  - **Part 2: How (Second Half)**
    - Developers plan how to deliver
    - Break items into tasks
    - Identify dependencies
    - Create Sprint Backlog
  - Timeline visual showing both parts
- **Visual:** Two-column layout, timeline

**Slide 8: User Stories Format**
- Title: "Writing Effective User Stories"
- Content:
  - **Standard Format:**
    - "As a [type of user]"
    - "I want [goal/desire]"
    - "So that [benefit/value]"
  - **Examples:**
    - Good: "As an environmentally-conscious user, I want to see my monthly carbon footprint trend, so that I can track my progress over time"
    - Bad: "Build carbon calculator" (no user, no value)
  - **INVEST Criteria:**
    - Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable
- **Visual:** Format template, good/bad examples side by side

**Slide 9: Acceptance Criteria**
- Title: "Defining Done for User Stories"
- Content:
  - **What Are Acceptance Criteria?**
    - Conditions that must be met for story to be considered complete
    - Written from user perspective
    - Testable and specific
  - **Format: Given-When-Then**
    - Given [context/precondition]
    - When [action/event]
    - Then [expected outcome]
  - **Example:**
    - Given I have entered my household size and monthly utility bills
    - When I click "Calculate Footprint"
    - Then I see my estimated monthly CO2 emissions in kg
- **Visual:** Format template with real example

**Slide 10: Story Points & Estimation**
- Title: "Story Points"
- Content:
  - **What Are Story Points?**
    - Relative measure of effort/complexity
    - Not time-based (not hours/days)
    - Team-specific (5 points means different things to different teams)
  - **What Affects Size?**
    - Complexity
    - Amount of work
    - Uncertainty/risk
  - **Common Scales:**
    - Fibonacci: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21
    - T-shirt: XS, S, M, L, XL
  - Key: Focus on relative sizing, not absolute
- **Visual:** Fibonacci sequence, comparison graphic

**Slide 11: Planning Poker**
- Title: "Planning Poker - Team Estimation"
- Content:
  - **How It Works:**
    1. Product Owner reads User Story
    2. Team discusses and asks clarifying questions
    3. Each person selects estimate card privately
    4. Everyone reveals simultaneously
    5. Discuss differences (highest & lowest explain)
    6. Re-estimate if needed until consensus
  - **Benefits:**
    - Prevents anchoring bias
    - Surfaces different perspectives
    - Engages entire team
  - **Scrum Master Role:** Facilitate, timebox discussions
- **Visual:** Planning poker cards, step-by-step process

**Slide 12: Estimation Practice**
- Title: "Let's Estimate Together"
- Content:
  - **User Story 1:**
    - "As a user, I want to enter my monthly electricity usage, so that it can be included in my carbon footprint calculation"
    - Questions: What size is this? 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13?
  - **User Story 2:**
    - "As a user, I want to see a detailed breakdown of my carbon footprint by category (transportation, energy, food, waste), so that I understand where my biggest impact comes from"
    - What size is this compared to Story 1?
- **Visual:** Stories in cards, interactive format

**Slide 13: Creating Sprint Goals**
- Title: "Crafting a Powerful Sprint Goal"
- Content:
  - **What Makes a Good Sprint Goal?**
    - One sentence that describes Sprint's purpose
    - Focused on value/outcome (not features)
    - Provides flexibility in implementation
    - Motivates the team
  - **Examples:**
    - ‚ùå Bad: "Complete stories 1-5"
    - ‚ùå Bad: "Implement carbon calculator"
    - ‚úÖ Good: "Enable users to understand their basic carbon footprint"
    - ‚úÖ Good: "Launch MVP that helps 100 users track their first sustainability goal"
  - **Template:** "Enable [users] to [achieve outcome]"
- **Visual:** Bad vs Good examples, template

**Slide 14: Sprint Goal Benefits**
- Title: "Why Sprint Goals Matter"
- Content:
  - **Benefits:**
    - Provides focus when priorities shift
    - Enables team to make tradeoff decisions
    - Creates shared purpose
    - Helps with mid-Sprint adjustments
  - **Example Scenario:**
    - Goal: "Enable users to calculate carbon footprint"
    - Mid-Sprint: API for utility data unavailable
    - Decision: Can we still achieve goal with manual entry? YES ‚Üí adapt
  - Without goal: Team stuck, unsure how to proceed
- **Visual:** Scenario flowchart, decision tree

**Slide 15: Capacity Planning**
- Title: "Understanding Team Capacity"
- Content:
  - **What Is Capacity?**
    - Amount of work team can complete in Sprint
    - Based on team size, Sprint length, and availability
  - **Factors Affecting Capacity:**
    - Planned time off / holidays
    - Other commitments (support, meetings)
    - Historical velocity
    - Team experience with technology
  - **Example Calculation:**
    - 6 developers √ó 2 weeks = 60 developer-days
    - Minus: ceremonies (6 days), support (4 days), learning (5 days)
    - Capacity: ~45 developer-days OR ~30-40 story points
  - **First Sprint:** Be conservative!
- **Visual:** Capacity calculation breakdown

**Slide 16: Sprint Backlog Creation**
- Title: "Building the Sprint Backlog"
- Content:
  - **What Goes In:**
    - Sprint Goal (the "why")
    - Selected Product Backlog Items (the "what")
    - Tasks to deliver them (the "how")
  - **Task Breakdown Example:**
    - Story: "User can enter electricity usage"
    - Tasks:
      - Design input form UI
      - Create backend API endpoint
      - Implement form validation
      - Write unit tests
      - Update user documentation
  - **Living Document:** Updated daily throughout Sprint
- **Visual:** Backlog structure diagram

**Slide 17: Sprint Planning Red Flags**
- Title: "Warning Signs in Sprint Planning"
- Content:
  - **Watch Out For:**
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Sprint Goal is vague or feature-focused
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Team commits to too much work
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è No discussion - PO just assigns work
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Planning takes longer than timebox
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Acceptance Criteria are unclear
    - ‚ö†Ô∏è Team hasn't refined backlog beforehand

    - Challenge over-commitment

## CHAPTER 7: DAILY SCRUM MASTERY (Slides 19-26)

**Slide 19: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "7"
- Title: "Daily Scrum Mastery"

**Slide 20: Daily Scrum Purpose**
- Title: "The Daily Scrum"
- Content:
  - **Purpose:** Inspect progress toward Sprint Goal and adapt Sprint Backlog
  - **Key Characteristics:**
    - 15 minutes, same time and place daily
    - For Developers (others can observe)
    - Focus on TODAY's work toward Sprint Goal
    - Not a status meeting!
  - **Outcome:** Plan for next 24 hours
- **Visual:** Clock emphasizing 15 min, daily calendar

**Slide 21: Traditional vs Modern Approach**
- Title: "Evolution of Daily Scrum Format"
- Content:
  - **Traditional 3 Questions:**
    - What did I do yesterday?
    - What will I do today?
    - Are there any impediments?
  - **Modern Approach:**
    - Start with Sprint Goal
    - What progress did we make yesterday?
    - What will we do today to move closer?
    - What stands in our way?
  - **Key Difference:** Focus on "we" (team) not "I" (individual)
  - Both formats valid - choose what works for your team
- **Visual:** Side-by-side comparison

**Slide 22: Daily Scrum Facilitation Tips**
- Title: "Facilitating Effective Daily Scrums"
- Content:
  - **As Scrum Master:**
    - Ensure it happens (same time/place)
    - Keep it to 15 minutes
    - Don't lead it - Developers run it
    - Remove impediments raised
    - Coach on staying focused
  - **Pro Tips:**
    - Stand up (keeps energy high, shorter)
    - Use Sprint Goal as north star
    - Move detailed discussions offline
    - Update Sprint Backlog during or after
    - Rotate facilitator within team
- **Visual:** Facilitation techniques list

**Slide 23: Common Daily Scrum Pitfalls**
- Title: "Daily Scrum Anti-Patterns"
- Content:
  - **What to Avoid:**
    - ‚ùå People reporting to Scrum Master
    - ‚ùå Detailed technical discussions
    - ‚ùå Solving problems in the standup
    - ‚ùå Reading from board mechanically
    - ‚ùå Taking longer than 15 minutes
    - ‚ùå Skipping when "nothing new"
    - ‚ùå Manager using it for status reports
  - **Solutions:**
    - Team talks to each other, not SM
    - "Let's discuss after" for deep dives
    - Focus on coordination, not problem-solving
    - Engage with Sprint Goal
    - Timebox strictly
    - Inspect & adapt is always valuable
    - It's NOT a status meeting
- **Visual:** X marks with corrections

**Slide 24: Handling Impediments**
- Title: "When Impediments Arise"
- Content:
  - **What's an Impediment?**
    - Anything blocking team from making progress
    - Examples: Tech issues, missing info, dependencies, team conflicts
  - **During Daily Scrum:**
    - Raise it briefly
    - Note it
    - Don't solve it in the meeting
  - **After Daily Scrum:**
    - Scrum Master works to remove it
    - Team self-organizes around it if possible
  - **Communication:**
    - Keep team updated on resolution progress
    - Escalate if needed
- **Visual:** Impediment workflow

**Slide 25: Daily Scrum Variations**
- Title: "Alternative Daily Scrum Formats"
- Content:
  - **Walk the Board:**
    - Start with rightmost column
    - Discuss each item moving left
    - Focus on Sprint Goal progress
  - **Yesterday's Weather:**
    - Review what shipped yesterday
    - Commit to what ships today
  - **Round Robin:**
    - Each person briefly updates
    - Quick and focused
  - **Silent Standup:**
    - Team updates board beforehand
    - Meet only to discuss blockers
  - Choose format that energizes YOUR team
- **Visual:** Different format icons

**Slide 26: Remote Daily Scrum Tips**
- Title: "Daily Scrum for Distributed Teams"
- Content:
  - **Best Practices:**
    - Use video (cameras on)
    - Share screen with Sprint Backlog
    - Use digital board (Jira, Azure, Trello)
    - Start on time even if people missing
    - Record for different timezones (optional)
  - **Challenges:**
    - Time zone differences
    - Connection issues
    - Harder to read body language
  - **Solutions:**
    - Rotate meeting time
    - Have backup plan
    - Encourage more verbal communication
- **Visual:** Remote work setup

## CHAPTER 8: SPRINT REVIEW (Slides 27-32)

**Slide 27: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "8"
- Title: "Sprint Review"

**Slide 28: Sprint Review Purpose**
- Title: "Sprint Review: More Than a Demo"
- Content:
  - **Purpose:** Inspect Sprint outcome and determine future adaptations
  - **What It IS:**
    - Working session with stakeholders
    - Collaboration on what to do next
    - Review of marketplace and product direction
    - Adaptation of Product Backlog
  - **What It's NOT:**
    - Just a demo or presentation
    - One-way status report
    - Acceptance meeting
  - **Outcome:** Revised Product Backlog for next Sprint
  - Timebox: Max 4 hours for 1-month Sprint
- **Visual:** Is/Is Not comparison

**Slide 29: Sprint Review Structure**
- Title: "Running an Effective Sprint Review"
- Content:
  - **Agenda (2-hour Sprint for 2-week Sprint):**
    1. Welcome & Sprint Goal reminder (5 min)
    2. Demo completed work (45 min)
    3. Discuss what didn't get done and why (10 min)
    4. Review Product Backlog status (15 min)
    5. Discuss marketplace changes (15 min)
    6. Collaborate on what to do next (25 min)
    7. Update Product Backlog order (10 min)
  - **Key:** Keep it interactive!
- **Visual:** Timeline with segments

**Slide 30: Stakeholder Engagement**
- Title: "Getting Stakeholders Engaged"
- Content:
  - **Before Sprint Review:**
    - Send calendar invite early
    - Share Sprint Goal and objectives
    - Set expectations: working session, not just demo
  - **During Sprint Review:**
    - Encourage questions throughout
    - Ask for feedback on each feature
    - Discuss real user scenarios
    - Capture new ideas for Product Backlog
  - **After Sprint Review:**
    - Share summary and action items
    - Follow up on commitments made
  - **Tip:** Make stakeholders feel ownership in product evolution
- **Visual:** Engagement cycle

**Slide 31: Demo Best Practices**
- Title: "Demoing Your Increment"
- Content:
  - **Do:**
    - ‚úÖ Show working software (not slides)
    - ‚úÖ Use realistic data and scenarios
    - ‚úÖ Have Developers demo their own work
    - ‚úÖ Explain value, not just features
    - ‚úÖ Invite stakeholder interaction
  - **Don't:**
    - ‚ùå Show incomplete work
    - ‚ùå Make excuses for bugs
    - ‚ùå Blame team members
    - ‚ùå Rush through without explanation
  - **If Something Breaks:**
    - Stay calm
    - Acknowledge it
    - Explain what was supposed to happen
    - Move on
- **Visual:** Do/Don't checklist

**Slide 32: Product Backlog Updates**
- Title: "Adapting the Product Backlog"
- Content:
  - **Based on Sprint Review:**
    - Add new items from stakeholder feedback
    - Re-order based on changed priorities
    - Remove items no longer valuable
    - Refine items for next Sprint
  - **Example:**
    - Stakeholder: "Users need dark mode"
    - Action: Add "Dark mode theme" to backlog
    - PO decision: Where does it rank?
  - **Remember:** Product Backlog is never complete - always evolving
- **Visual:** Backlog adaptation workflow

## CHAPTER 9: SPRINT RETROSPECTIVE (Slides 33-40)

**Slide 33: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "9"
- Title: "Sprint Retrospective"

**Slide 34: Retrospective Purpose**
- Title: "The Heart of Continuous Improvement"
- Content:
  - **Purpose:** Plan ways to increase quality and effectiveness
  - **Core Questions:**
    - What went well?
    - What could be improved?
    - What will we commit to improve?
  - **Outcome:** Actionable improvements for next Sprint
  - **Timebox:** Max 3 hours for 1-month Sprint
  - **Attendees:** Scrum Team only (safe space)
  - **Key:** This is where the magic happens - embrace it!
- **Visual:** Continuous improvement cycle

**Slide 35: Retrospective Formats**
- Title: "Popular Retrospective Techniques"
- Content:
  - **Start-Stop-Continue:**
    - What should we start doing?
    - What should we stop doing?
    - What should we continue doing?
  - **Mad-Sad-Glad:**
    - What made us mad/frustrated?
    - What made us sad/disappointed?
    - What made us glad/happy?
  - **4 L's:**
    - Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For
  - Rotate formats to keep fresh!
- **Visual:** Format comparison cards

**Slide 36: Sailboat Retrospective**
- Title: "Sailboat Retrospective (Visual Method)"
- Content:
  - **Draw a sailboat:**
    - **Island (Goal):** What are we trying to reach?
    - **Wind (Helping):** What's pushing us forward?
    - **Anchor (Holding Back):** What's slowing us down?
    - **Rocks (Risks):** What dangers should we avoid?
  - **Activity:**
    - Team adds sticky notes to each area
    - Discuss and group themes
    - Prioritize improvements
  - Great for visual/creative teams!
- **Visual:** Sailboat diagram with labeled parts

**Slide 37: Facilitating Retrospectives**
- Title: "Scrum Master's Role in Retros"
- Content:
  - **Before:**
    - Choose format (vary it)
    - Prepare materials
    - Set psychological safety
  - **During:**
    - Create safe space (Prime Directive)
    - Keep time
    - Ensure everyone participates
    - Focus on systems, not people
    - Drive toward action items
  - **After:**
    - Document improvements
    - Add them to Sprint Backlog
    - Follow up on progress
  - **Prime Directive:** "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time..."
- **Visual:** Facilitation cycle

**Slide 38: Making Improvements Stick**
- Title: "From Insights to Action"
- Content:
  - **Common Problem:** Great discussion, no change
  - **Solution:**
    - Limit improvements to 1-3 per Sprint
    - Make them specific and measurable
    - Assign ownership
    - Add to Sprint Backlog as tasks
    - Check progress in next Retro
  - **Example:**
    - ‚ùå Vague: "Improve communication"
    - ‚úÖ Specific: "Create team Slack channel for daily blockers; check it twice daily"
  - **Track It:** Keep a visible list of past improvements
- **Visual:** Action item template

**Slide 39: Retrospective Anti-Patterns**
- Title: "Retro Pitfalls to Avoid"
- Content:
  - ‚ùå Blame game (finger-pointing)
  - ‚ùå Same format every time (boredom)
  - ‚ùå Manager attending (lack of safety)
  - ‚ùå No action items decided
  - ‚ùå Action items never implemented
  - ‚ùå Dominated by loudest voices
  - ‚ùå Too long or rushed
  - **Solutions:** Safe space, variety, actionable commitments, balanced participation
- **Visual:** X marks with solutions

**Slide 40: Retrospective Prime Directive**
- Title: "Creating Psychological Safety"
- Content:
  - **The Retro Prime Directive:**
    - "Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand."
  - **Why It Matters:**
    - Creates safe space for honesty
    - Prevents blame culture
    - Focuses on systems, not people
  - **Read It:** Start every Retrospective with this
- **Visual:** Quote format, emphasized text
