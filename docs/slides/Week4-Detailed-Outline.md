## CHAPTER 14: SCRUM AT SCALE (Slides 6-20)

**Slide 6: Chapter Divider**
- Large number "14"
- Title: "Scrum at Scale"
- **Visual:** Full-screen gradient background

**Slide 7: Why Scale?**
- Title: "When Should You Scale Scrum?"
- Content:
  - **Good Reasons to Scale:**
    - Product is too big for one team
    - Need to deliver faster
    - Different skill sets required
    - Geographic distribution
  - **Bad Reasons to Scale:**
    - "More people = faster delivery" (usually false!)
    - Following industry trends
    - Avoiding hard conversations
    - Organizational politics
  - **Remember:** One team is simpler. Scale only when necessary.
  - **Key Question:** "Can we simplify the product instead?"
- **Visual:** Good vs bad scaling reasons

**Slide 8: Scaling Challenges**
- Title: "What Makes Scaling Hard?"
- Content:
  - **Communication Complexity:**
    - 1 team: 3 roles
    - 3 teams: 9 roles
    - Communication paths explode!
  - **Common Challenges:**
    - Dependencies between teams
    - Integrating work from multiple teams
    - Shared resources
    - Consistent Definition of Done
    - Product Backlog management
    - Conflicting priorities
  - **Brooks' Law:** "Adding people to a late project makes it later"
- **Visual:** Communication complexity diagram

**Slide 9: Scaling Frameworks Overview**
- Title: "Frameworks for Scaling Scrum"
- Content:
  - **Popular Frameworks:**
    - **Nexus** (Scrum.org) - 3-9 teams, one product
    - **LeSS** (Large Scale Scrum) - 2-8+ teams
    - **SAFe** (Scaled Agile Framework) - Enterprise-wide
    - **Scrum@Scale** - Variable scaling
  - **Today's Focus:** Nexus
    - Created by Scrum creators
    - Minimal framework
    - Extends Scrum, doesn't replace it
    - Ideal for 3-9 teams
  - **Note:** PSM I covers basic scaling concepts, not frameworks in depth
- **Visual:** Framework comparison table

**Slide 10: Nexus Framework Introduction**
- Title: "What Is Nexus?"
- Content:
  - **Definition:**
    - Framework for scaling Scrum to 3-9 teams
    - All teams work on ONE product
    - Preserves Scrum values and principles
  - **Key Principle:**
    - Each team runs Scrum
    - Additional coordination layer
    - Focus on integration
  - **When to Use:**
    - Single product, multiple teams needed
    - Teams working on related features
    - Need coordinated delivery
- **Visual:** Nexus overview diagram

**Slide 11: Nexus Integration Team**
- Title: "The Nexus Integration Team (NIT)"
- Content:
  - **Composition:**
    - Product Owner (one for all teams)
    - Scrum Master(s)
    - Integration-focused team members
  - **Accountable For:**
    - Coaching teams on Nexus
    - Integration issues visibility
    - Removing integration impediments
  - **What They Do:**
    - Facilitate Nexus events
    - Ensure integrated Increment
    - Help teams resolve dependencies
    - Coach on technical practices
  - **What They Don't Do:**
    - ‚ùå Do the integration work
    - ‚ùå Make product decisions
    - ‚ùå Manage the teams
- **Visual:** NIT structure diagram

**Slide 12: Nexus Events Overview**
- Title: "Nexus Events"
- Content:
  - **Nexus Sprint Planning:**
    - All teams plan together
    - Identify dependencies
    - Agree on integration points
  - **Nexus Daily Scrum:**
    - Representatives from each team
    - Discuss integration issues
    - 15 minutes
  - **Nexus Sprint Review:**
    - Combined demo
    - Integrated Increment
    - All stakeholders
  - **Nexus Sprint Retrospective:**
    - Cross-team improvements
    - Then individual team retros
- **Visual:** Nexus events timeline

**Slide 13: Nexus Sprint Planning**
- Title: "Nexus Sprint Planning"
- Content:
  - **Part 1: Nexus Sprint Planning (All Teams Together)**
    - Product Owner presents Product Goal
    - Discuss top Product Backlog items
    - Identify dependencies
    - Each team selects work
  - **Part 2: Individual Team Sprint Planning**
    - Each team plans their Sprint
    - Considers dependencies identified
    - Creates Sprint Backlog
    - Coordinates with other teams
  - **Output:**
    - Each team has Sprint Backlog
    - Dependencies are visible
    - Integration points agreed
    - Nexus Sprint Goal
- **Visual:** Two-phase planning process

**Slide 14: Managing Dependencies**
- Title: "Dependency Management Strategies"
- Content:
  - **Types of Dependencies:**
    - **Knowledge:** Team A needs info from Team B
    - **Resource:** Teams need same specialist
    - **Technical:** Team A's code blocks Team B
  - **Strategies:**
    - Make dependencies visible early (planning)
    - Cross-team pairing
    - Shared components
    - Service interfaces
    - Regular integration
  - **Warning Signs:**
    - Teams waiting on each other
    - Last-minute integration
    - Finger-pointing
  - **Prevention:** Architecture, CI/CD, communication
- **Visual:** Dependency types and solutions

**Slide 15: Nexus Daily Scrum**
- Title: "Nexus Daily Scrum"
- Content:
  - **When:** Before individual team Daily Scrums
  - **Who:** Representatives from each team (often 1-2 people)
  - **Duration:** 15 minutes
  - **Focus:**
    - Integration issues from yesterday
    - Integration work planned for today
    - Impediments affecting multiple teams
  - **NOT:**
    - Detailed status updates
    - Problem-solving session
    - All team members attending
  - **Then:** Each team has their own Daily Scrum
- **Visual:** Nexus Daily Scrum flow

**Slide 16: Integration Practices**
- Title: "Technical Practices for Integration"
- Content:
  - **Essential Practices:**
    - **Continuous Integration (CI):**
      - Teams integrate frequently (daily/hourly)
      - Automated builds and tests
      - Catch integration issues early
    - **Shared Definition of Done:**
      - All teams follow same DoD
      - Includes integration criteria
      - Non-negotiable
    - **Shared Code Standards:**
      - Consistent coding practices
      - Common architecture
      - Shared repositories
    - **Automated Testing:**
      - Unit tests
      - Integration tests
      - End-to-end tests
  - **Without these:** Scaling fails
- **Visual:** Integration practices pyramid

**Slide 17: Nexus Sprint Review**
- Title: "Integrated Review"
- Content:
  - **What's Different:**
    - One review for all teams
    - Demo INTEGRATED Increment
    - Not separate team demos
  - **Structure:**
    - Demonstrate integration
    - Show how features work together
    - Stakeholder feedback on whole product
    - Update Product Backlog
  - **Common Mistake:**
    - Each team demos separately
    - Integration issues hidden
    - Stakeholders see disconnected pieces
  - **Best Practice:**
    - Show user journeys across teams
    - Highlight integration successes
- **Visual:** Integrated vs separate demos

**Slide 18: Nexus Retrospective**
- Title: "Two-Level Retrospective"
- Content:
  - **Nexus Retrospective (First):**
    - Representatives from all teams
    - Focus on cross-team issues
    - Integration improvements
    - Nexus process improvements
  - **Team Retrospectives (After):**
    - Each team has own retro
    - Focus on team-specific issues
    - Can address Nexus action items
  - **Key:** Cross-team improvements first, then team-level
  - **Common Themes:**
    - Communication channels
    - Dependency management
    - Integration frequency
    - Shared tooling
- **Visual:** Two-level retrospective structure

**Slide 19: Scaling Anti-Patterns**
- Title: "What NOT to Do When Scaling"
- Content:
  - ‚ùå **Component Teams:** Teams organized by architecture layer (UI, backend, DB)
    - Creates dependencies
    - Slows delivery
    - ‚úÖ Better: Feature teams (cross-functional)
  - ‚ùå **Separate Product Owners:** Each team has own PO
    - Conflicting priorities
    - No unified vision
    - ‚úÖ Better: One PO for the product
  - ‚ùå **Integration Sprint:** Last Sprint for integration
    - Delays feedback
    - Hides problems
    - ‚úÖ Better: Continuous integration
  - ‚ùå **Scrum of Scrums:** Just for status updates
    - Becomes meeting overhead
    - ‚úÖ Better: Nexus Daily Scrum with focus
- **Visual:** Anti-patterns with corrections

**Slide 20: Scaling Wisdom**
- Title: "Keys to Successful Scaling"
- Content:
  - **Do:**
    - ‚úÖ Start simple, scale when needed
    - ‚úÖ Invest in technical practices
    - ‚úÖ Feature teams, not component teams
    - ‚úÖ One Product Owner, one Product Backlog
    - ‚úÖ Frequent integration
    - ‚úÖ Shared Definition of Done
  - **Remember:**
    - Scaling amplifies problems
    - Fix issues in one team before scaling
    - More teams ‚â† faster delivery
    - Communication is key
  - **Quote:** "Scrum is hard enough with one team. Don't scale until you must."
- **Visual:** Success factors checklist

**Slide 30: About PSM I**
- Title: "Professional Scrum Master I Certification"
- Content:
  - **What It Is:**
    - Entry-level Scrum Master certification
    - From Scrum.org (Ken Schwaber's organization)
    - Globally recognized
    - No renewal required (lifetime certification)
  - **What It Tests:**
    - Scrum framework knowledge
    - Scrum Master accountabilities
    - Scrum theory and principles
    - Practical application
  - **Value:**
    - Validates your knowledge
    - Career advancement
    - Demonstrates commitment
- **Visual:** PSM I badge and credentials

**Slide 31: Exam Format**
- Title: "PSM I Exam Details"
- Content:
  - **Format:**
    - 80 multiple-choice questions
    - 60 minutes
    - Online, monitored
    - Open book (Scrum Guide allowed)
  - **Passing Score:**
    - 85% (68 out of 80 correct)
  - **Cost:**
    - $150 USD
    - Two attempts with one payment
  - **Languages:**
    - English primary
    - Translation tool available
  - **No Prerequisites:** Anyone can take it
- **Visual:** Exam statistics infographic

**Slide 32: Exam Strategy**
- Title: "How to Approach the Exam"
- Content:
  - **Time Management:**
    - 60 minutes = 45 seconds per question
    - Don't get stuck
    - Flag uncertain questions
    - Review flagged at end
  - **Before the Exam:**
    - Read Scrum Guide 3+ times
    - Take Scrum Open until scoring 95%+
    - Rest well, be fresh
  - **During the Exam:**
    - Read each question carefully
    - Look for absolute words (always, never, must)
    - Eliminate obviously wrong answers
    - Choose best answer (not perfect answer)
  - **After:**
    - Review flagged questions
    - Trust your preparation
- **Visual:** Exam strategy checklist

**Slide 33: What to Memorize**
- Title: "The 3-5-3 Rule + Timeboxes"
- Content:
  - **3 Roles:**
    - Product Owner
    - Scrum Master
    - Developers
  - **5 Events:**
    - Sprint (container)
    - Sprint Planning
    - Daily Scrum
    - Sprint Review
    - Sprint Retrospective
  - **3 Artifacts + Commitments:**
    - Product Backlog ‚Üí Product Goal
    - Sprint Backlog ‚Üí Sprint Goal
    - Increment ‚Üí Definition of Done
  - **Timeboxes (1-month Sprint):**
    - Sprint: 1 month max
    - Planning: 8 hours max
    - Daily Scrum: 15 minutes
    - Review: 4 hours max
    - Retrospective: 3 hours max
- **Visual:** 3-5-3 visual + timebox table

**Slide 34: Accountability Questions**

- Content:
  - **Product Owner Accountable For:**
    - Product Backlog management

  - **Scrum Master Accountable For:**
    - Scrum effectiveness

**Slide 35: Common Exam Traps**
- Title: "Watch Out For These Tricks"
- Content:
  - **Trap 1: Absolute Language**
    - "Scrum Master MUST always..."
    - Scrum has few "musts" - be suspicious of absolutes
  - **Trap 2: Best Practice Confusion**
    - Question asks what's in Scrum Guide
    - Answer describes common practice (but not in Guide)
  - **Trap 3: Role Confusion**
    - "Product Owner assigns tasks to Developers"
    - FALSE - Developers self-manage
  - **Trap 4: Definition of Done**
    - When unclear, refer to organizational standard
    - If none exists, team creates one
  - **Trap 5: Sprint Cancellation**
    - Only Product Owner can cancel Sprint
    - Only when Sprint Goal becomes obsolete
- **Visual:** Warning signs for each trap

**Slide 36: Scrum Guide Language**
- Title: "Know the Scrum Guide Terminology"

  - **Key Terms:**

    - ‚ùå "Development Team" ‚Üí Developers

**Slide 37: Sprint Event Questions**
- Title: "Common Sprint Event Questions"
- Content: Five Q&A examples:
  
  **Q1:** Who attends Sprint Planning?
  - **Answer:** Entire Scrum Team (PO, SM, Developers)
  
  **Q2:** Who can cancel a Sprint?
  - **Answer:** Only the Product Owner
  
  **Q3:** When does a Sprint end?
  - **Answer:** When the timebox expires (never early!)
  
  **Q4:** Who facilitates the Daily Scrum?
  - **Answer:** Developers (Scrum Master ensures it happens but doesn't lead it)
  
  **Q5:** When can the Sprint Goal be changed?
  - **Answer:** Never during the Sprint (only when Sprint is cancelled)

**Slide 38: Artifact Questions**
- Title: "Product Backlog & Sprint Backlog"
- Content: Common questions:
  
  **Q:** Who owns the Product Backlog?
  - **Answer:** Product Owner is accountable
  
  **Q:** Who can add items to Product Backlog?
  - **Answer:** Anyone can suggest, but PO decides what goes in
  
  **Q:** Who owns the Sprint Backlog?
  - **Answer:** Developers
  
  **Q:** Can Sprint Backlog change during Sprint?
  - **Answer:** Yes! Developers update it as they learn
  
  **Q:** Must all Product Backlog items be estimated?
  - **Answer:** No - only items likely to be worked on soon
  
- **Visual:** Backlog ownership diagram

**Slide 39: Definition of Done Questions**
- Title: "Definition of Done Scenarios"
- Content:
  
  **Q1:** Who creates the Definition of Done?
  - If org has standard ‚Üí Use it (minimum)
  - If not ‚Üí Scrum Team creates it
  
  **Q2:** Can DoD change?
  - Yes! Can be improved but never lowered
  
  **Q3:** What if work doesn't meet DoD?
  - Cannot be released
  - Returns to Product Backlog
  
  **Q4:** Multiple teams, multiple DoDs?
  - NO! Multiple teams on one product = ONE shared DoD
  
  **Q5:** Is DoD the same as acceptance criteria?
  - No! DoD = quality standard for ALL work
  - Acceptance criteria = specific to one PBI
  
- **Visual:** DoD decision tree
